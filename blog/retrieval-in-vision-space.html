Retrieval in Vision Space

September 2025 · Gyuho Shim

Classic document retrieval treats every page as a bag of words. Run OCR, chunk into paragraphs, embed the text, and retrieve. This pipeline is well-understood — and it quietly throws away half the information on a typical page. Column alignment, table borders, font weight, spatial proximity of numbers to their labels: none of it survives OCR into a text string.

A newer line of work asks a different question: what if the retrieval model read the page as an image, the way a human does? This post covers two papers from my summer 2025 seminar that make this concrete — one that proposes the vision-based retrieval architecture, and one that stress-tests it against OCR-based alternatives under degraded scan conditions.

1. ColPali: Efficient Document Retrieval with Vision Language Models (ICLR 2025)

1.1 The Standard Pipeline and Its Failure Mode

The standard RAG pipeline for document retrieval is: 

PDF → OCR → text chunks → text embedder → dense index → nearest-neighbor search

On clean, purely textual documents this works fine. But real-world document collections — financial filings, academic posters, product manuals — are visually rich. A figure caption next to a bar chart, a table where column headers span two rows, a flowchart with directional arrows: OCR extracts the characters and loses everything else. The retrieved chunk says "Q3 Revenue: 4.2B" with no indication that the number appeared in a row labeled "Asia Pacific" inside a table titled "Regional Breakdown."

The alternative — rendering each page as an image and using a vision-language model to understand it — was dismissed for years as too slow for indexing at scale. ColPali changes that calculus.

1.2 Architecture: ColBERT × PaliGemma

ColPali's architecture is the intersection of two prior ideas:

  • ColBERT's Late Interaction: instead of compressing a document into one vector, keep a separate vector per token. Matching is done by a max-sim operation between query tokens and document tokens.
  • PaliGemma's visual tokenization: a Vision Transformer (ViT) splits each page image into a grid of 16×16-pixel patches and produces one embedding per patch — a "bag of image patches," analogous to a bag of word tokens.

Combined, ColPali encodes a query text into a sequence of token embeddings E_q and each document page image into a sequence of patch embeddings E_d, then scores them with the Late Interaction (MaxSim) function: 

LI(q, d) = Σᵢ max_j <E_q^(i) | E_d^(j)>

For every query token, find the most similar document patch; sum those maxima across all query tokens. The dot product <·|·> is cosine similarity after L2 normalization.

This is significant in two ways. First, query tokens can attend selectively to the most relevant regions of the page, rather than being forced through a single bottleneck embedding. Second, all document patch embeddings are pre-computed and stored at index time — retrieval is just a MaxSim lookup, not a full VLM forward pass.

1.3 ViDoRe: Visual Document Retrieval Benchmark

The paper introduces ViDoRe (Visual Document Retrieval Benchmark), a benchmark of 10 retrieval datasets spanning diverse document types: scientific figures, medical scans, administrative documents, and more. The primary metric is NDCG@5 — normalized discounted cumulative gain at rank 5.

ColPali's results on ViDoRe:

System NDCG@5 Indexing speed
Standard OCR pipeline 0.66 7.22 s/page
ColPali (PaliGemma-3B) 0.81 0.39 s/page

ColPali achieves +15 NDCG@5 points over the OCR baseline while being 18× faster to index. The speed advantage comes from eliminating the OCR step entirely — a single ViT forward pass over the page image replaces the OCR + chunking + embedding pipeline.

1.4 Why Late Interaction Works for Pages

A page image at 448×448 pixels (ColPali's input resolution) is divided into 28×28 = 784 patches. Each patch becomes one entry in E_d. The query "What was Q3 revenue in Asia Pacific?" might have 9 tokens. MaxSim finds, for each query token, the single most-relevant patch — e.g., the token "Asia Pacific" will max-sim to the patch covering the relevant table row, not to a patch covering an unrelated figure on the same page.

The single-vector alternative must compress all 784 patches into one number — spatial layout is irrecoverably mixed together. Late Interaction preserves the spatial decomposition all the way through scoring.

1.5 Limitations

ColPali's main cost is storage: 784 vectors per page instead of 1. For a 1M-page corpus, a 128-dim index stores 1M × 784 × 128 × 4 bytes ≈ 400 GB. This is manageable but meaningfully larger than a single-vector index. Quantization (binary or int8) and dimension reduction via Matryoshka training are the standard mitigations.

2. Lost in OCR Translation? (ACM DocEng 2025)

2.1 Motivation: Do VLMs Actually Beat OCR Under Scan Degradation?

ColPali's benchmark results come from digitally-born PDFs. Real enterprise document archives contain scanned physical documents — sometimes scanned at low DPI, with ink bleed, skew, or physical damage. Under these conditions, does a VLM-based retriever still dominate, or does a robust OCR pipeline with explicit degradation handling come back?

"Lost in OCR Translation?" is a systematic evaluation paper that constructs a controlled degradation benchmark and measures both families of systems under each condition.

2.2 DocDeg: The Degradation Benchmark

The paper introduces DocDeg, a benchmark of document pages at four degradation levels:

  • Level 0 — Digital copy: native PDF rendering, no degradation
  • Level 1 — Perfect scan: clean physical scan at high DPI
  • Level 2 — Imperfect scan: noise, slight skew, moderate DPI
  • Level 3 — Severely degraded: heavy noise, low DPI, staining or physical damage

The progression simulates the actual distribution of documents in large enterprise archives, where levels 0–1 represent recently-digitized material and levels 2–3 represent older physical records.

2.3 Systems Compared

System Type Notes
ColQwen2 7B VLM-based retriever ColPali variant on Qwen2-VL 7B backbone
Nougat OCR-based (specialized) Scientific document OCR + text embedder
Llama 3.2 OCR OCR-based (LLM) LLM-powered OCR + text embedder

2.4 Findings

The results are counterintuitive at level 0 but consistent across levels 1–3:

  • Level 0 (digital): Nougat achieves the highest retrieval performance — specialized OCR on clean digitally-born PDFs is very effective. ColQwen2 is competitive but does not win.
  • Levels 1–3 (scanned/degraded): Llama OCR consistently outperforms both alternatives, including the VLM-based system. Nougat degrades sharply: its specialized model was trained on clean scientific PDFs and generalizes poorly to scan artifacts.
  • ColQwen2 is the most consistent across degradation levels — it never hits the highs of Nougat at level 0 or Llama at levels 1–3, but it also never collapses. The VLM is more degradation-robust than Nougat but is consistently outperformed by a strong LLM-based OCR pipeline.

2.5 Interpretation

This finding has a clear reading: VLM-based retrieval is not a universal improvement over OCR-based retrieval. ColPali/ColQwen2's strength is on visually complex documents where layout carries meaning that OCR discards. When the bottleneck is scan quality rather than layout complexity, a capable LLM doing OCR — which has been trained on noisy text from the web and is inherently robust to character-level noise — is a strong baseline.

The practical implication for system design: document archive composition matters. If the corpus is primarily scanned physical documents at variable quality, LLM-powered OCR + text retrieval is the pragmatic choice. If the corpus is visually-rich digitally-born PDFs, VLM-based retrieval offers meaningful gains. Most real archives contain both.

3. Takeaways

  • Late Interaction is a better match for document pages than single-vector retrieval. ColPali's MaxSim over patch embeddings preserves spatial structure that a pooled embedding discards, yielding +15 NDCG@5 on ViDoRe at 18× faster indexing than OCR pipelines.
  • VLM-based retrieval does not universally dominate. Under scan degradation, a capable LLM OCR system (Llama 3.2) outperforms ColQwen2 7B, because OCR robustness is the binding constraint — not layout understanding.
  • Specialized OCR models are brittle. Nougat is best-in-class on clean digital PDFs and worst-in-class on degraded scans. Domain-specific tools need domain-matched data.
  • Retriever architecture choice should be driven by the corpus. The two paradigms are complements, not substitutes. A production system might route based on predicted document quality.
